Ealing Studios: A Testament to British Cinema

Founded in 1902, Ealing Studios holds the distinction of being the oldest production studio still in operation in the UK. From its humble beginnings with just three stages, it has expanded to over five stages, ranging from 1,000 to 10,000 square feet, and has become home to some of the UK's most iconic films. Ealing Studios' journey reflects the evolution of British cinema, navigating wartime challenges, producing post-war classics, and nurturing future stars.

Wartime Challenges and Collaboration

Ealing Studios was one of Britain's most successful production houses during the 1940s and 1950s, producing films that resonated with British values and life. However, during World War II, the studio faced pressure from the government, including Winston Churchill, who deemed some films too liberal or incendiary. These films, often dealing with sensitive topics like Nazis in Britain or POW camps, were initially suppressed to prevent damaging troop morale or public indifference towards the war effort. Despite these challenges, Ealing managed to release these films, much to Churchill's displeasure.

Adapting to wartime needs, Ealing began collaborating with the government to produce films that supported the war effort. These films, which depicted military life and heroism, were not profitable for the studio. The government provided resources such as men, weapons, and vehicles, but Ealing only received their initial investment plus a small percentage of box office returns, with most royalties going to the Treasury. Despite this unequal relationship, Ealing continued to produce impactful war films.

The studio itself was not a high-priority target for German bombers, but it still experienced occasional threats. Ealing employed a staff member to watch for enemy aircraft and sound the alarm, halting filming and sending staff to the air raid shelter in the basement.

The Golden Years

Post-war, Ealing repositioned itself with a series of successful comedies that reflected the changing British society. The studio's first color film, Saraband for Dead Lovers (1948), marked a significant milestone. This romantic period melodrama about a doomed royal love affair showcased Ealing's versatility and ambition.

Ealing became a coveted destination for aspiring directors, producers, and writers. Barry Letts, who later directed and produced Doctor Who, began his career at Ealing. Other alumni went on to work on classics like Lawrence of Arabia and The Avengers.

The studio also launched the careers of renowned actors like Alec Guinness and Peter Sellers. Audrey Hepburn's first substantial role was in The Queen of Spades (1947), where she caught the eye of Hollywood despite Ealing's initial oversight of her potential. This film played a crucial part in her journey to stardom, culminating in her iconic role in Roman Holiday.

Safety and Challenges

Ealing Studios was known for its creative environment but was less diligent about safety. Alec Guinness experienced several near-fatal accidents, including a near-drowning incident during "Kind Hearts and Coronets" and a fall on the set of "The Lavender Hill Mob." Despite these risks, Guinness's contributions were pivotal to Ealing's success, although he eventually demanded higher insurance coverage for dangerous stunts.

The Power of Unions

Unions held significant power at Ealing Studios in the 1950s. Every staff member had to join one of the three unions: ETU for electricians, NATKE, and ACT for the art and camera departments. While unions were meant to protect workers, many felt they were more of a nuisance. The catch-22 of needing a union card to work in films, but requiring film work to obtain a union card, created additional challenges.

The End and Legacy of Ealing Studios

In 1955, Ealing Studios was sold to the BBC for £350,000, but the brand name continued under managing director Michael Balcon. The BBC used Ealing extensively, producing successful content throughout the 20th century. The studio remained active, hosting productions like the Simon Pegg comedy "Shaun of the Dead" and the "St. Trinian’s" franchise revival. It also became home to the Met Film School, London’s leading provider of practical filmmaking courses. Independent television companies, including those producing "Downton Abbey," also utilized Ealing's facilities.

Ealing Studios' partnerships with major production companies like MGM were short-lived but contributed to its rich history. The studio's remarkable journey from its inception to its enduring legacy epitomizes the resilience and evolution of British cinema. Despite challenges, Ealing maintained its reputation for creativity and innovation, influencing modern filmmaking and education. Ealing Studios stands as a symbol of artistic excellence, preserving its heritage while embracing new opportunities in the ever-evolving world of cinema.
