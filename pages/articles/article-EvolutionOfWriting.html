The Evolution of Writing: From Sumerian Clay to Digital Bytes

Between the years 3500 BC and 3000 BC, a phalanx of Sumerian geniuses invented a system for storing and processing information outside their brains, one that was custom-built to handle large amounts of mathematical data. This may sound like a science fiction discovery which hitherto has been sequestered away from civilization. However the data-processing system described is called ‘writing’.

The Sumerian Invention: Birth of Writing

The Sumerian writing system embodied simplicity. The earliest symbols of arithmetic were created by combining two types of signs, which were pressed in clay tablets. One type of sign represented numbers. There were signs for 1, 10, 60, 600, 3,600 and 36,000. (The Sumerians used a combination of base-6 and base-10 numeral systems.) This ostensible archaic system may have a legacy dating back to the earliest epoch of human history, however, the Sumerian’s base-6 system bestowed on us several important legacies, such as the division of the day into twenty-four hours and of the circle into 360 degrees. The Evolution to Cuneiform and Beyond

As one civilization ended and another began, more cultures adopted the vernacular. Between 3000 BC and 2500 BC more and more signs were added to the Sumerian system, gradually transforming it into a full script that we today call cuneiform. At roughly the same time, many precocious cultures were developing their proprietary text. Egyptians developed another full script known as hieroglyphics. Hieroglyphic drawings have led to rampant speculation among archaeologists as generations have failed to decipher the complete lexicon. Other full scripts were developed in China around 1200 BC and in Central America around 1000–500 BC but also waned into obscurity. Ancient religious texts like the Hebrew Bible, the Greek Iliad, the Hindu Mahabharata and the Buddhist Tipitika all began as oral works before they were transcribed into their native lexicon. The Advent of Numerical Script and Its Modern Relevance

A critical step was made sometime before the ninth century AD, when a new partial script was invented, one that could store and process mathematical data with unprecedented efficiency. This partial script was composed of ten signs, representing the numbers from 0 to 9. Confusingly, these signs are known as Arabic numerals even though they were first invented by the Hindus (even more confusingly, modern Arabs use a set of digits that look quite different from Western ones). But the Arabs get the credit because when they invaded India they encountered the system, understood its usefulness, refined it, and spread it through the Middle East and then to Europe. When several other signs were later added to the Arab numerals (such as the signs for addition, subtraction and multiplication), the basis of modern mathematical notation came into being.

The Impact of Writing on Human Thought

For thousands of years, humans have greatly benefited from the creation and dissemination of a human data transmission system. Writing allows for the storage and preservation of knowledge over time. It enables the transmission of information and ideas across generations, ensuring that valuable insights and experiences are not lost. Each language has evolved throughout the ages as the contemporaries adapt it to elucidate, proselytise and encourage perpetual learning and understanding. Through this medium of communication, great literature has come into existence, venerated works have inspired revolutions and religious scriptures have guided billions. Writing is still developing in the modern day. From base 6 to base 10 to now zeros and ones; writing was born as the maidservant of human consciousness, but is increasingly becoming its master. Our computers have trouble understanding how we talk, feel and dream. So instead we are teaching humans to talk, feel and dream in the language of numbers, which can be understood by computers.

In this ever-evolving journey from Sumerian clay to digital bytes, writing has not only served as a conduit for our thoughts but also as a bridge between the past and the future, shaping our understanding of the world and our place in it.
