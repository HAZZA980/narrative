From Columbus to Colonialism: Tracing the History of New York City

New York is one of the most salient cities in the world. From its grandiose nature and the rambunctious prestige, however it is important to note how the city became the important symbol which it has become. Over its tumultuous history, it has been occupied by the Spanish, French, Dutch and the British. First, we need to know the history of the first settlers in America.

In 1492, Christopher Columbus was funded by the Spanish monarchs, Ferdinand II of Aragon and Isabella I of Castile, to burgeon the hegemony of the Spanish Empire. It is erroneous to state that Columbus ‘discovered’ America as this is a Eurocentric term ignoring the history of the indigenous tribes who inhabited the continent for thousands of years before. The correct phrase would be arrived or encountered when he first landed in the Caribbean islands, specifically the Bahamas during his voyage.

Columbus was accredited with the acknowledgment of finding new land west of Europe however it is worth noting that the reason why it isn’t eponymously named after him is due to an indiscretion by a cartographer in 1907. Martin Waldseemüller was a German cartographer, tasked with producing a comprehensive map of the world known as the "Universalis Cosmographia". This was to include all the recent explorations of the previous two decades. Waldseemüller had read about the voyages of explorer, Amerigo Vespucci, deducing that he was the first to find the continent. Vespucci had indeed travelled to America twice in 1499 and 1502 becoming a talented navigator around America. In light of this Waldseemüller titled the new territory as America in honour of Vespucci.

As the Renaissance period commenced, the Spanish Empire continued to proliferate. The Spanish monarchy continued their reign and their mission to conquer all. They headed south to subjugate the Aztec and the Inca Empires in the first few decades of the 16th century. The empire continued to grow in both power and size making the Spanish Empire one of the biggest history has recorded. Because the strict territories were not enforced, other countries could take land without prior warning. This either resulted in capitulation or war. The Spanish focused their colonial efforts in South American countries and the south-west of America because they had already excavated the minerals from their previous odysseys. The Dutch, known for their vast trade empire, colonised coastal towns primarily for economic reasons and to expand their empire.

In 1609, Henry Hudson, an Englishman working for the Dutch East India Company, explored the region and claimed it for the Dutch. The Dutch saw the potential for profitable fur trade with the Native American inhabitants. In 1624, the Dutch West India Company established the colony of New Netherland, with its capital at New Amsterdam (modern-day New York City), as a trading post and agricultural settlement. This was a lucrative port as it controlled passage across the Atlantic Ocean.

The Dutch trading connections were vastly important which was the impetus why the British assumed control of the city from the Dutch in 1664. The most valuable commodity was the trade routes which the city provided. The Dutch were tenacious however after much bloodshed, the British fleet usurped control. The colonisation of New Amsterdam was spearheaded by the Duke of York, later known as King James II. The city was formally renamed New York from that point onwards. The status quo was beneficial and profitable for both the indigenous people and for the British until tensions arose between the inhabitants, particularly the Lenape tribes, over land ownership, trade disputes, and cultural clashes. This ultimately led to conflicts such as the Esopus Wars and the Peach Tree War in the late 17th century. Additionally, the British imposition of taxes and regulations further strained relations with the local populations. Geopolitical changes in Europe had a prodigious impact on the British controlled New York. As competition between England and the Netherlands for dominance in trade and colonisation perpetuated, the indigenous people were caught in the crossfire further consolidating the rift between the parties. These pernicious factors culminated in the American Revolution in 1776 leading America to fight against colonialism and for freedom.

In conclusion, the journey of New York City from its early days as a Dutch trading post to its pivotal role in the American Revolution exemplifies the complexities of colonisation, cultural clashes, and the quest for freedom. As we reflect on its rich and diverse history, it becomes evident that New York's evolution mirrors the broader narrative of human civilization, marked by triumphs, conflicts, and the relentless pursuit of liberty. Today, as a global metropolis, New York stands as a testament to resilience, innovation, and the enduring spirit of its inhabitants, reminding us that the legacy of the past continues to shape the present and inspire the future.
