THE METAMORPHOSIS - Franz Kafka (1915)

Introduction:

The Metamorphosis is one of Franz Kafka’s most revered books. The Czechoslovakian writer is known for many of his horror stories leading to this particular one being banned in a number of conservative countries such as China and Russia. Written in 1915 during the height of the first world war, Kafka became well known for his imagination and his unique writing.

Summary:

The Metamorphosis begins with Gregor Samsa, a travelling salesman, waking up one morning to discover that he has transformed into a giant insect-like creature. Initially shocked and disoriented, Gregor struggles to come to terms with his new form and its implications. As he attempts to adjust to his situation, he grapples with feelings of alienation and despair, particularly as he observes his family's reactions to his transformation. His family, consisting of his parents and his sister Grete, initially tries to care for him, but they quickly become repulsed and increasingly neglectful. Gregor's inability to communicate effectively exacerbates the growing tension between him and his family. Despite his efforts to maintain his humanity, Gregor's physical condition continues to deteriorate, and he becomes increasingly isolated from society. Ultimately, Gregor's existence becomes burdensome to his family, and they come to see him as a hindrance. The novella concludes with Gregor's death, after which his family experiences a sense of relief and newfound freedom.

Themes:

In this short novella, we are exposed to a myriad of themes. A reverent number when we take into account the ephemeral length of this book. First of all, Gregor’s transformation into a giant insect leads to isolation from society. This becomes a constant theme throughout the book; he must adapt to his solitude. He is still in contact with his family, however, his transformation has prodigiously affected his relationship with his parents and sister. His sister especially, becomes a caring, sympathetic figure, tending to approach Gregor with an open mind and supports him in his condition. His mother, first is sequestered from Gregor by his sister and father as they think that it would be too upsetting for a mother to see her child in that way. They arrange to supervise Gregor’s interaction with his mother on weekly visits to monitor her reaction. The father is initially shocked, analogous to the reaction of Gregor’s sister, however soon becomes unperturbed and wants Gregor to leave. The novella delves into the complex relationships and the shifting dynamics as they grapple with the transformation and their coming to terms with the implications which it has on their lives. The final theme which I am going to focus on is the guilt and responsibilities which each family member feels incumbent to act upon. The moral dilemmas which Gregor faces, speaks to his noble character as he doesn’t want to become a burden for his family. He formerly worked as a travelling salesman and was able to contribute his wage to the family’s upkeep. He must not live the rest of his life being cared for as he is not able to work, nor provide any real useful qualities to help his family.

Writing Style:

As this book was written in the early 20th century, the lexicon used is simple, and easy to follow. It is not a children’s book per say, however the language used is not complex therefore would be suitable for all audiences. We are introduced to Gregor, the main character, who undergoes the metamorphosis. Instead of assigning individual appellations to each character, Kafka chooses to denote the characters in subordinate roles by their relationship to Gregor, such as Father, Mother, Sister, and cleaner. This keeps the fulcrum of the story on Gregor with the family adding additional context. Because of the unique nature of this book, it is understandable that the novella is written in third person so we are able to grasp the thoughts and feelings of the family. This adds another layer of detail and development to Gregor’s transformation as we are shown how it affects the family instead of having to assume. As this story is relatively short and has a paltry number of characters, Kafka comprehensively describes the environment and the actions of Gregor using brackets to embed more details into the story, enabling us to vividly imagine what Kafka is showing us.

Personal Reflection:

This book intrigued me from the start. The first sentence is “One morning, as Gregor Samsa was waking up from anxious dreams, he discovered that in bed he had been changed into a monstrous verminous bug”. This is one of the best openings I have ever read. There are no labyrinthine metaphors or superfluous details. Kafka is blunt and direct. This grabbed my attention and maintained it throughout the entire novella. The relationships Gregor shares with his sister and parents after the transformation become increasingly fraught as they grapple with the unfamiliar circumstances and endeavour to determine how long they must endure the new status quo. The strain which Gregor has unwillingly put on the rest of his family is almost palpable through Kafa’s writing making the narrative a compelling page turner. The ephemeral life span of a bug expectedly catches up with Gregor leading to his death. I didn’t think that after the turmoil and pressure which has resulted from the metamorphosis, the family would be poignant in grieving Gregor’s death, however I was mistaken. Even despite the incredulous reactions of the family, they were still mournful of the death of their beloved son and brother. They remembered him as he was instead of the creature he became.

Conclusion:

Franz Kafka’s novella, The Metamorphosis provides a stark insight into loneliness, family bonds and guilt. Published amidst the chaos of World War I, Kafka's masterpiece continues to captivate readers with its timeless themes. As we reflect on Kafka's personal background and the historical context of his work, we gain a deeper appreciation for the enduring legacy of this literary gem. This book end ambiguously as it doesn't provide a definitive resolution to the family's situation or the meaning behind Gregor's transformation. After Gregor's demise, his family feels a sense of relief, and they start to plan for their future. The story ends with the family contemplating their prospects, suggesting a new beginning for them after the traumatic events they've endured. However, Kafka leaves much open to interpretation, allowing readers to contemplate the existential and psychological themes raised throughout the novella.
