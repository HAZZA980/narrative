How I Got Here

Ever had a fancy bottle of wine stashed away for the perfect occasion but the perfect occasion has never arrived. That was what this idea of studying abroad was for me. Something which I could look forward to but never actually having to do. The idea was a reason for me to remain sanguine about living with anxiety. Having a goal which one day I would be able to achieve. Living abroad is something which I always found intriguing, but I would never want to leave my privileged life in England. I am a creature of habit you see and the thought of leaving that meticulously crafted routine makes me feel nauseas even thinking about it.

In January when I needed to make a decision on where I wanted to go. I initially picked Charleston, South Carolina as my first choice with Stockholm, Sweden as my backup. At the time, I was blinded by the thought of living in America and living the stereotypical life of an American college student. Again, this was me creating a fantasy in my head and not actually thinking about the logistics and forgetting about my old nemesis, anxiety. It can raise its ugly head in the most normal of circumstances. Playing football, doing pub quizzes, even just thinking about the future. For you lucky people who have not had mental health issues, the easiest way to understand is to pretend you have a stone in your shoe which you just cannot get rid of. You can manoeuvrer it to more comfortable positions and at times you may even forget it is there, but just when you think you are doing well, your heel stands on it wipes out a the progress you have made. Back to square one.

Anyway back to America. Charleston seemed like such a glamorous town on the east coast. I was looking at fraternities and computer science modules however that quaint little dream disappeared when university politics got involved. It wasn’t made clear to me until after I submitted my choices to De Montfort that College of Charleston was actually being revoked from the list of available partner universities because the relationship had broken down between the aforementioned universities, leaving me with a fait accompli of Stockholm. Off to Sweden it is then.

That decision was made over 8 months ago when I still had over half of my second year remaining. In the months preceding, it was frequently in the back of my mind, normally accompanied by sudden waves of nausea. The brain is the most powerful muscle in the body which makes it a formidable adversary when it turns against us. Cognitive behaviour therapy equipted me with strategies to regain control and to recognise triggers as they emerged. Anxiety is all in the mind. Positive mind equals positive outcome. Give me strength. If 15 year old me knew what his future held, I’m sure he would alter his views on mental health and put more effort in at school. Of course, when battling with the mind, you are going to lose a battle from time to time.

During the rare instances when I’ve yielded to my nemesis, I adopt a two-pronged approach. First, I strive to diminish its power, then I attempt reasoning with it. Reminiscing about a previous trip to Bilbao the previous year in which I experienced the same trepidation gave me some confidence. If these strategies falter, my final recourse is distraction. Music, walks, focus on a coding puzzle – anything to draw my thoughts elsewhere. Gradually, these thoughts dissipate and I return to reality. It’s worth noting that before the Bilbao journey, I stoically managed occasional doubts as they were no match for my impenetrable positive mindset. Upon reaching the Basque county, most of my fears ebbed away, leaving room for genuine enjoyment. Forming lasting friendships and confronting only fleeting moments of anxiety. I should emphasize that this trip spanned only a week and maybe should not have been the sole catalyst for me opting for an entire year abroad. Nevertheless, it marked a pivotal victory in my ongoing battle, strengthening my mind for the future journeys out of my comfort level.
