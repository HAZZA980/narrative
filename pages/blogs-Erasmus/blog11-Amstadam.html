Amsterdam - A City of Change?

My latest excursion to a European citadel was Amsterdam, the city of sex, drugs, prostitutes, and dalliances. Its reputation certainly preceded it, so of course, I was intrigued. I wanted to see and learn as much as I could in the ephemeral time I had there. My obligatory walking tour was the logical place to start. Led by a flamboyant, gay Englishman around the lascivious streets of Amsterdam was exactly the paragon I was expecting from the city of sin. With the expedient practices omnipresent throughout the city, I assumed the denizens would be unperturbed by the clandestine activities; and from what I was told, I assumed correctly. This, as expected, draws in millions of euros from tourism; however, it has reached the point where Amsterdam residents who have resided there for decades feel detached from their home city.

After the pandemic, a collective attitude emerged in Amsterdam. With tourism declining, the city embraced a sense of tranquillity and reclaimed some reverence. However, as the world returned to its status quo ante, Amsterdam resumed its ostensible role as the European capital for sex and drugs. Recognizing the need for a change in perception, the Dutch government has proposed relocating the red-light district to a new neighbourhood on the periphery of the city. This move aims to allow the once venerated old town to regain its historical beauty without being sullied on a daily basis.

Once this plan is realised and coffee shops are subjected to stricter regulations regarding the distribution and sale of cannabis, Amsterdam will be liberated from the expedient industries it has been known to condone. However, with these changes, the new image may lack a certain raison d'etre to attract tourists. Personally, I enjoy walking in the parks, appreciating local art, and visiting museums, but these are not tourist attractions that can sufficiently recoup the lost revenue from the old ways. The Dutch government faces a prodigious task in making their ambitious plans come to fruition. Call me a pessimist, but I sincerely believe that after these implemented changes, the only gravitas Amsterdam will hold is that it is the capital city of the Netherlands.

As the evenings drew in, I embarked on the obligatory walk that all tourists must take around the red lights of Amsterdam. I endeavoured to avoid making eye contact with the sex workers as they attempted to entice passersby into their domain. The red light district is situated in the old town of Amsterdam, amidst a tableau of historical buildings, churches, and town halls. It appears as though these venerable structures have been marred by the addition of doors carved into their facades to cater to the needs of the sex workers. As someone with a more conservative mindset, I find it disheartening that such a clandestine industry thrives in this cultural hotspot of a beautiful city.

I feared that two days is too short to cover all of the landmarks and cultural spots which I had noted to visit. However, much to my incredulity, I felt like I had assimilated a great deal of history and accrued a tapestry of knowledge from my time in the Dutch capital. My only regret is missing out on the Van Gogh gallery. Due to the spontaneous nature of my trip, I neglected to do my due diligence; therefore, I was perplexed when informed of the four-day waiting list for entry. Is it a city which I would visit again? Probably not. This is not because I did not enjoy my time in Amsterdam, but because of the intimate nature of the city, I have already covered a vast portion and am inclined to think another trip would be superfluous. I wouldn’t be experiencing anything which I haven’t seen before. As self-deprecating as it sounds, once you’ve seen one canal, you have seen them all. Europe has many more cities, and to be succinct, in my opinion, I thought Amsterdam was unfulfilling when compared to my trips to Vienna or Prague.
