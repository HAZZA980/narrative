How Bad is Communism, Really?

Now, this may seem like a contentious issue to debate, so first, I think I should explain what got me thinking about this subject. I was on the metro heading into the centre of Stockholm to enjoy the early signs of spring weather in Sweden. As usual on the metros, there are mendicants soliciting money, preachers trying to proselytise new believers, or advocates trying to inculcate their chosen cause. Most are anodyne, which doesn’t warrant a blog post; however, this morning, there was an aberration.

'Any communists on the train?' is the phrase I heard. Immediately, my ears pricked up. This man was promoting a newly formed party which is accumulating support from various factions around the region. It is not unusual to hear individuals expressing their religious beliefs or political predilections so overtly, yet openly asking if there are any communists on the train almost seemed incendiary. I can admit that I know very little about the party ideology and their motivations despite their auspicious development from Marxism; however, the immediate association with communism is negative. As a result, many people will be thinking about the Cold War, Stalin, the USSR, and modern-day China – all of which are conspicuous indicators of the negative connotations surrounding communism.

But then it occurred to me, if this guy is openly campaigning for the renaissance of the communist party, are the core fundamentals still relevant? And has it previously failed in the past because of despotic rulers operating under the guise of the ideology?

Communism as an ideology has its roots in the works of philosophers and economists like Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels. Their work laid out the principles of communism in "The Communist Manifesto," and was published in 1848. This manifesto was a call to action for the working class to rise against the oppressive conditions imposed by capitalism. That line alone is a reason for concern as it implicitly is a call to arms, but stay with me.

In the manifesto, the historical development of the class system is described along with the rise of capitalism, which they argue exploits the labour of the proletariat for the benefit of the bourgeoisie. They propose that the inevitable result of this exploitation will be a revolution in which the working class will overthrow the capitalist system and establish a classless society based on common ownership of the means of production. The manifesto itself is a fascinating read and is many ways more succinct than the holy bible, in the aspect that only one set of principles are outlined instead of providing conflicting arguments for each side of issues. The key points in the manifesto include the abolition of private property, the centralization of the means of production in the hands of the state, and the establishment of a dictatorship of the proletariat as a transitional stage towards a stateless, classless society. All of which seems like an intricate social experiment which has its benefits but also, as history has shown us, its downfalls too. So now we know the fundamentals of communism, we can now derive the positives which are provided from this ideology. First of all, the elimination of class inequality could provide a peaceful utopia with the state distributing the wealth evenly across all inhabitants, regardless of their socioeconomic background. Sounds good right, until we dive into the complexities of human psychology. Work gives us purpose and the possibility of earning more money in order to live more comfortably may benefit us. Capitalism personified. With state controlled monopolies, communism can quickly evolve into totalitarianism.

To get away from this sinister train of thought. An overwhelmingly positive aspect which I can think of is communist societies often prioritise providing basic necessities such as healthcare, education, housing, and employment to all citizens as fundamental rights rather than privileges. In the UK, we already have a taste of this with the NHS and public school so this would have a much greater effect in America where healthcare is exhaustionate (from what I’ve heard). By employing this, it can lead to improved living standards and reduced poverty rates for the population. Point for communism.

I read that when there is no competition among peers, there is an emphasis on cooperation and solidarity. So with the abolishment of capitalism, the importance of camaraderie among individuals and communities rather than competition and individualism is inflated. This fosters a sense of community and mutual support, which can contribute to social unity and well-being. Again reiterating that social utopia what I was talking about earlier.

Because everything is state run, it means that there is still a government in place to distribute the wealth. Even though there may be a president or prime minister, the people have more control in governance and policy-making. Now, one the one hand, this could lead to increased political participation and empowerment of the masses, or it could be detrimental to the countries GDP and services because even though everyone is equal in this egalitarian society, I’m positive that not everyone will be sufficiently educated to make the powerful decision which only officials in the upper echelons of government should be required to make.

Although there are obviously some pitfalls when implementing this system and clearly some wrinkles which need to be straightened out, but that doesn’t explain how countries like the USSR, Laos, China and North Korea have slipped so far into imperialism that it is difficult to come back from that. Well, many communist states have faced challenges such as corruption, authoritarianism, economic inefficiency, and human rights abuses, which have undermined the realisation of these ideals. Also, the feasibility and desirability of communist principles remain subjects of debate among scholars and policymakers.

However, it's important to note that the implementation of communist ideology in various countries throughout history has often deviated from the ideals laid out in the manifesto. Despotic rulers have often manipulated communist ideology for their own authoritarian purposes, leading to totalitarian regimes and widespread human rights abuses.

The Soviet Union under Joseph Stalin, for example, implemented a form of communism known as Marxism-Leninism, which deviated significantly from the original principles of Marxism. Similarly, countries like China under Mao Zedong and North Korea under Kim Il Sung adopted variations of communism that veered away from Marx's vision.

So, while the core principles of communism as laid out in "The Communist Manifesto" may have noble intentions of achieving social equality and justice, the historical record of communist regimes has been marred by authoritarianism, repression, and economic inefficiency. This complex history has contributed to the negative connotations associated with communism in many parts of the world. So to conclude a blog post in what has been an education for myself, if communism is executed correctly, the principles may work and benefit all however it is far more likely that an imperious leader will get corrupted with the power bestowed on him and stray into his own ideology which benefits him over the people. Succinctly, communism is bad.
