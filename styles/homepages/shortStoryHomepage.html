My Short Story Ideas

In my Creative Writing course, I was tasked with writing a 3000 word story. That's it. No direction, characters, themes or allegory mentioned. 'Write a story which is intriguing' were the words of my lecturer. I didn't know where to start. I was reading 'The Writers Tale' at the time by Russel T. Davies so alot of my thoughts my have stemmed from an idea he mentions in his book. Alternatively, if they are poor, they are most probably my own. I had two months to write my story and these blogs take you through my (at times) labyrinthine thoughs and ideas. You can read the finished short story here

Chapter 1 - The Start

22/02/2024

I don’t expect this to be a refined work of art. This is just for me to vent and brainstorm ideas. I think it will be interesting to see what avenues I explore and which story lines I abandon for various reasons. To explain briefly, I need to write a 3000 word short story for my Creative Writing course at Stockholm University. I don’t have any further instructions. Write a short story, how hard can that be, right? The deadline is the 20th of April so I have lots of time to think of a story, write it, rewrite it and doubt myself at every turn. I am not worried because even if I write a totally crap story, it’s still a learning experience and this gives me the opportunity to get some (hopefully) constructive criticism. Either way, it’s going to be a journey whatever the outcome.

First of all, let me get my excuses out of the way. I don’t write much fiction. Or any fiction for that matter. I write more articles on books which I have read in order to consolidate my knowledge on a particular subject area. The Boston Tea Party, signal transmissions, even network protocols. I find that this helps me revise and simultaneously improves my writing. I don’t even read much fiction anymore. I started reading consistently during the pandemic and that is when I read the most amount of fiction. Anything that was recommended, either by Amazon or Richard & Judy. I really wasn’t fussy. As I started widening my vocabulary, I moved on to biographies and a company. They are sort of fictions however it’s all true. I was building a computer at the time and discovering a passion for Computer Science. As a side note, that was the impetus for me deciding to go to university. These books about Apple, Facebook, Twitter, Netflix, and any other technology company became my inspiration for choosing a career. These books gradually became my preferred genre, debasing almost all fiction. I still chose to read Shakespeare and the classics. Dickens, Austen, Wells, Dumas. I strongly think that anyone who has an interest in reading should read these classics from centuries ago. This helped us all understand the different vocabulary and the writing styles of each author. Also to appreciate their skills as a lexicographer. Their unique styles, their imaginations. Personal favours include H.G. Wells, The Time Machine and Alexander Dumas’ The Three Musketeers. I could happily reread these novels but with so much literature to choose from, I think it is a waste of time rereading stories which we know the endings of.

Getting back to the short story, my first hurdle is choosing a topic. A rather big hurdle. A completely blank slate. Where do I start? Something contemporary? Historical? Who should my character be? Male, female, straight, gay, bi, teachers, scientists, artists, royal, poor. How does one decide? I think many people write from what they know. In the seminars, it was implicitly stated to write about something you like. This is obvious, but still incredibly vague. Does this mean writing about hobbies, or goals? Do I still have modern day problems or do I start writing about Sci-Fi creatures and aliens and see where it takes me.

Chapter 2 - Waiting for a lightbulb Momement

There would be five members of the gang- each with a specific skill and arcane backstory. I wanted to have their stories intertwined in some way because I think the more connections a character has, the more they are relatable. This can sometimes correlate to the quality of the story as well. The twist, which I think every story should have, is that the house which they are robbing is actually the father of the gang leader. Oooo, father/son conflicts. I’m not sure about the quality of this but every avenue is worth exploring at the moment.

If this was a film, it would start with an action packed first scene of them breaking into the perimeter and gripping the audience with writing one liners which portray the characteristics of each gang member. Then it would go back to the main protagonist. Smithers. How’s that for a name of an international art thief. Do people instinctively think of the assistant to Mr Burns from the Simpsons? Maybe I need to rethink the name. Then the story will continue showing Smithers recruiting the rest of the team. As they get on with planning the heist on the art collectors house, it will become apparent why each of them has opted to join Smithers. This will include themes like money troubles, gambling debts, greed, the rush of breaking the law. This is coming along nicely but I still have a lot of wrinkles that I need to iron out. Should the gang all be focused on equally? What about a love interest? One of the gang could have grown up with Smithers and has been besotted with him for years. She will do anything for him including break the law. This doesn’t exactly create a strong female character which is all the rage right now. Smithers is doing this because it is his father’s art collection but what if his impetus for stealing the art is because he has been cut out of his father’s will. Or his father has remarried and his new wife is wanting to sell the art, so Smithers is actually stealing the art to keep them in the family. Maybe it doesn’t have to be that complicated. He could just be doing it because he is a thief. More things to think about. I need to sleep on it and see what I come up with tomorrow.

Chapter 4 - Stymied by the Word Count?

The labyrinthine events which I want to meticulously knot together would likely make this a short novella rather than a short story. I won’t be able to get the scrupulous details which will accurately explain why each character is taking part in the heist if I shorten it. I have spoken to some friends from my course about this and they have come to the same inference. I could reduce the gang from five down to three in an attempt to streamline the story. They could all be the art collectors' children and their father’s new wife is attempting to sell the art. I am going down that route again. I’m still not 100% invested in the idea of a heist as the story. I don’t watch many films like Six Underground, or Red Notice or the ocean films and think, that is a truly wonderful story. Yes, they are entertaining films but the stories are not gripping. It also helps having huge actors and budgets to provide the grip for the audience. Since I’m lacking in both of these aspects, that’s why this story needs to be based on a strong foundation where the writing is the sole purpose for gripping the readers. I’m going back to the drawing board, because the art heist just isn’t sitting well with me.

Chapter 5 - Why is it Hard to Think of a Story?

01/03/2024

I thought I would have a huge epiphany when I had my million dollar idea. Something which would fill me with euphoria and erethism. Something that I could put my finger on and say THAT’S IT! The story which really stands out from the monotony of mediocre ideas. Characters which I can picture before I have even written a word describing them. Their inclinations towards different circumstances. The chemistry they have with each other. The dialogue which brings them to life. I have been racking my brain hoping for an olive branch for one of these ideas. I don’t know how writers come up with their ideas. I have read articles and books on authors explaining their process. Stephen King, Francis Prose, Russell T Davies. There isn’t a conclusive blueprint on how to create a good script. The overwhelming conclusion is, despite the common fallacy, most ideas are contrived rather than naturally created. I don’t know how much I agree with this. Ideas are created from the various stimuli which we are exposed to and then exaggerated and developed into fictional stories. Our experiences can spark a seemingly simple idea to flourish into an intricate story with spaceships and laser guns with swamp creatures and little green aliens and a good and a dark side. Sorry, sorry. I watched Star Wars earlier and it’s evidently still fresh in my mind. There are so many variables which can tilt the scales from era defining novels to stories which were promising but missed the mark by targeting a niche demographic, or lost the intrigue of the audience part way through. The latter fade away to be forgotten whilst the former are remembered for centuries. Of course I am hoping for neither of these. Anything this isn’t branded as crap by my classmates is the nadir which I will gladly accept.

Chapter 6 - A Plausible Idea

02/03/2024

What about Kings and Queens? Of course, there is a plethora of fictional works which has exhausted almost every avenue of ingenuity so my story will have to be more about the execution rather than the originality. First of all, I was thinking of a historic tale based on the Queen of England. Of course, I’m British so it makes sense for me to write about our monarchy. The King has died of a mysterious malaise, leaving the throne to his only daughter – Queen X. I haven’t thought of her name yet. I want it to be something sweet and yet powerful. If there is such a name. Something with gravitas which antagonists will come to fear. I also need it to be something that resembles nobility. A name which people will hear and think, yes, I can tell that is a royal by her name. Elizabeth, Mary, Cleopatra. Hmm. Queen Sarah. No, forget that. That’s awful. I’ll come back to it later. The Queen is facing a revolution as her subjects are revolting against her fathers tyrannical rule. With the King dead, now is the time to fight to ensure that they don’t live under a similar rule. This sounds similar to the French revolution but for my story, I’m thinking about the Queen slaughtering the proletariat, or at least those who have raised arms to the Queen. Is this too imperious? How would the reader react to this? No story is 100% awful if the underdog, against all odds, secures a victory. Proper David vs Goliath style.

From a writer's point of view, it’s almost like picking low hanging fruit. That’s why I’m more inclined to go for a dark twist at the end to shock the reader. Maybe I lure the reader in even further by having the Queen portrayed as a stark aberration from her father. Cordial and simpatico nature. She gains the trust of her subjects until she snaps. Her benevolence was only a facade for her to flush out her fathers adversaries. She executes the anti-royalists and begins her reign of terror. Actually, this is turning into a rather interesting plot. The quasi-reformation of the Monarchy makes her subjects believe that this is the start of a golden age only for her to follow in her father’s footsteps. Will this make the reader dislike the story when I turn the Queen’s likeable character into the antagonist. If done correctly, at best, this makes the story venerated. A story which readers can say, it's a good plot although not really the ending in which we wanted. This comes back to something which I read in Russell T Davies’ book A Writer’s Tale. He says something like, the audience is not a contributor to the story, they are merely a bystander. It is not a democracy, it is a dictatorship. The writer’s way is supreme. Of course, it helps having the stellar reputation which Russell has. A career of successful projects. Can neophyte writers such as myself take this risk. Potentially creating a congenial character which the readers fall in love with and suddenly turn them into a imperious dictator. I need to decide whether to take this risk.

Chapter 9 - Where to Start?

05/03/2024

Where do writers start? Do they have an ending in sight and work backwards to figure out the story? Plugging the gaps as they go. Do they focus on the character relationships and write blindly adding challenges and protagonists as they go? From what I’ve read from other writers, this is all correct. And this is all wrong. There is not a definitive guide. Writing is subjective in nature. It all depends on the story which you are trying to tell. The fulcrum of it. Whatever that may be. Find out what will be at the heart of the story and then build everything else around it. Maybe a central theme, lust, greed, love. Is the story focusing on the characters and their reactions to the events unfolding around them. Does the character's thoughts and feelings play a prominent role in how we want the readers to respond. Is talking in first person or third person more impactful? Alternatively, will the plot dictate the direction in which the story flows, irrespective of what the characters are feeling. So many variables that I need to thread together in order to create a story. Most authors play it safe and choose a centre point which ideas just proliferate from. Some of the more talented authors can make their stories more intricate and have many central points. They can switch narratives, and themes, character POVs whilst keeping the story flowing in a linear fashion. It is like spinning plates, keeping track of all the characters movements, their problems and visceral behaviours. Add another piece to the overall jigsaw of the story every time the narrative is switched.

The very best stories culminate in a grand climate in which all the loose ends are tied up in a nice little bow and the readers are left thinking “of course, how did I not see that coming”. Personally, I’m not much of a juggler. I hope to provide a riveting story which keeps the audience gripped. Books like George Orwell’s nineteen eighty-four had the most gripping last hundred pages I have ever read. Winston had been captured and he was being tortured by big brother. It was an initial shock as I didn’t expect them to be caught in the fashion which they were. Furthermore, the ending left me feeling unfulfilled. Sure, it was a congruous end, which perfectly captured the essence of big brother and the social status quo but I was expecting Winston to die with his dignity and steadfast probity. Sticking it to the man even with his dying breath. But no. Final words of the novel, Winston says “I love big brother.” A total capitulation of his character and acquiesce to the state. No revolution, no coup d’etat. A memorable ending to a classic novel. I can’t put my finger on it, ultimately making it so resplendent. If I could, I would definitely try to replicate it. Unfortunately, I’ll have to attribute it to the genius of George Orwell.

Chapter 10 - Touching on Reality

06/03/2024

What if the death of the King was the catalyst for war between England and Scotland. I am choosing Scotland because the Scots have been wanting independence so who am I to deprive them of this. When the King of Scotland comes to pay his respect to Queen Annabelle, his visit can come with the caveat of War if the Queen didn’t stand aside for the King to take the reigns. I want the Queen to be a young Queen because I think this always creates a better image in the reader's head. Queen Elizabeth II, Queen Mary, Lady Jane Grey. All came on the throne at young ages and faced scepticism but proved to be adroit leaders. I want Queen Annabelle to be the same. I have been for a walk around Stockholm and I’ve been building Queen Annabelle in my head. Not what she looks like or her style of dress but her character. The fresh air seems to have sparked some light in my mind of darkness. Poetic? Maybe not for the story. Annabelle is the King's young daughter, maybe 22 or 23, which means she’s well educated but her youth can introduce a witty and borderline cheeky side to her. I must use the correct adjectives, because I don’t want readers to think that she is arrogant or rude. Instead of spoon feeding the reader the character traits of the Queen, I think it would resonate more if I had her interact with commoners or other nobles and show what she is like so the readers can make up their own opinions. I think it’s important that she is kind and avuncular, congenial to her subject type of person. Ultimately, I want her to be relatable. I want readers to have a strong connection to her so they care about her fate. Even if the rest of the story is poor, I want a strong character representation. I might still decide to kill her off, following the Orwellian approach.

Chapter 12 - Ebbs and Flows

08/03/2024

I been getting excited now, the ideas are flowing like a downhill stream. I have been thinking about the first exchange between King S and Queen Annabelle. I don’t want it to be an all out war straight away. I want the audience to get an insight into both characters and the motifs that drive them. The battle should happen towards the end of the book but not be the main part. I am keen on the King and Queen having an amiable exchange almost immediately after the King’s death. He will come to offer his condolences however during the course of the exchange, King S makes subtle hints about taking the throne because of Queen Annabelle’s inexperience and inequities. Because he’s cunning renegade, he makes it sound like he is doing her a favour because running a nation is stoic work. I want to write it like King S is giving Annabelle an excuse to extricate herself from the bourdon of the throne. Keeping the conversation polite, Annabelle understands the true nature of this request and refuses the offer. This is where we can see Annabelle’s sophistry as she retorts with witty remarks about the state of Scotland’s unruly state since King S’s coronation.

As this is the first meeting, this just sets the stage and shows us what the characters stance are. Sort of like dogs, sniffing their intellectual arseholes. Apologies for the vulgar language but I thought it was a poetic parable. I haven’t nailed down the specifics of the conversation as I’m still drafting but this is certainly a positive start. This does make a nice opening for the Kings declaration of war. He’s offered her the carrot so then it’s time for the stick. The King goes back to Scotland, summons the ecclesiastical nobles and beguiles them into providing funds for the war to come. This is a minor detail which could be cut out but I think it would be beneficial for the reader to see the machiavellian nature of the King and it further adds to his character. Things are starting to come together nicely.

Chapter 13 - How do I know if I've Struck Gold?

10/03/2024

The more I think about this story, the more I get excited but also more stressed. Writing can be incredibly revealing in one's psych. Is a story about a King's lust for power truly interesting for the average reader. What draws a person to a book? What do they think of? Will Kings and Queens be interminably linked to upper class snobbery. I need people to have a tabula-rasa when reading this story but that is never going to happen unless they suddenly have a debilitating accident and lose their memory. The more I think about it, the more sceptical I get. It is hard to have people relate to royals because almost none of us have lived that life, or even appropriated that life for a short time. I am basing this story around Queen Annabelle who has grown up in line to the throne, privileged by the circumstances of her birth and been encompassed in all the appurtenances of royal life. And I’m expecting people to have this visceral connection to her character? With the quality of my writing, I am either going to portray her as a commoner, at best a bourgeoisie, or if miraculously, I succeed and have her walk and talk like a royal, she will be too far-fetched to be brought to life. Part of me wants to start again to write something more easier but writing isn’t supposed to be easy. It is so subjective that even the very concept is nebulous to us. If I do stick to this plot line, I may well change direction to make some of it easier to relate to. Look at me, I’m saying this like I am in line for the throne and all you peasants don’t know the luxury of my life!

If only someone could actually be honest and tell me if this is indeed a bit crap. I have no idea if I am driving up a blind alley but only time will tell. Besides, when you describe a story on paper or to a friend, it all sounds crap anyway. The characters haven’t been developed, the particular setting details haven’t been established, I’m describing the bare structure of the story. The King of one country wants to annex another country. Sounds like … oh yeah, every other war ever to happen in all of existence. The only practical advice I have been given which I think has a meagre effect is “the verisimilitude of the story depends more on the execution rather than the actual plot”. Simply put, everyone could write a story based on the same plot but the quality of the writing is what separates the weak from the good. The best thing to do when I’m feeling like this is to drop everything and come back tomorrow. Hopefully, I fall back into exuberance over pessimism.

Chapter 14 - A Vengeful King or a Benevolent King

11/03/2024

I can’t get King S out of my head. His back story, how he is the king of Scotland, the reason for his aloof character. Well strap in because I’ve had a maelstrom of thoughts about our frightful antagonist in this tale. I’ve mentioned already that Scotland has gained their independence, however, what if King S was actually not royalty. He became the self appointed king after his successes in the Scottish war for independence. He was first a soldier who led the mutinous coup d’etat against the incumbent King of Great Britain, (aka. Annabelle’s father). He was a prominent figure in the sedition against England and rose through the ranks to power and status. This was the impetus for his megalomania and the raison d’etre why he wants to usurp Queen Annabelle. Being born a commoner, he gained a venerated reputation, rising to the head of the Scottish Army. When they gained independence, he self appointed himself as King of Scotland because he was the most powerful commander in the army. The Scottish people didn’t oppose as they trusted King S from his auspicious army record. Fast forward to present day, the King of England died and King S wants to extend his power by subjugating the country that once ruled Scotland. Also it is a personal vendetta against the old King and his family. Perhaps focusing on the King's back story would be better as this seems to open more doors. I need to decide how much of this I am going to explicitly mention and what I can subtly hint at giving the reader the tacit information about King S’s past.

Also just by the way, my process has guided me, it is evident that this story will be affirmatively character based instead of ploy based. I am dwelling far too deeply into how the respective monarchs will behave and interact with each other to then shift the focus to the plot and take a circumlocution approach to the characters. I am not sure if this will make my task easier or harder. I suppose it will only become clear when I actually get around to writing the story. From a dogmatic view, plot driven stories seem easier and there are less fastidious details invested in the characters, therefore it is not a problem or a hindrance if the reader doesn’t connect with the characters. Plot driven stories focus more on the setting and the descriptive nuisances of what is happening. The characters are often tertiary and only speak when it drives the plot forward. I am already imagining witty lines of dialogue between my two main characters to drop them to tertiary elements. I love them too much for that. But who do I love the most to base the story around. It is clear that King S will be the antagonist because he is the one instigating the conflict however he does seem like the more interesting character at the moment. Rags to riches stories always carry more weight in the grand scheme of this. They also seem to be easier to relate to and empathise with. Wait, does this mean I should kill King S off to attain the bigger shock to the reader. I guess I don’t have everything figured out yet, but these characters are certainly becoming more like real people rather than works of fiction.

Chapter 15 - The Power of a Name

12/03/2024

I’ve already established the importance of a name. Above all else, it must fit the character. It removes the gravitas of a heroine if they are called Shannon or Courtney. No offence if you are called one of these names, it is just when I’m writing, I find it hard to picture the little boy in trouble shouting “Help, Help me Shannon ''. To figure out King S’s moniker, I’ve been searching for Scottish proletariat names from the sixteenth century, hoping one will jump out at me. I built King S to be such a complex character: war hero, megalomaniac, politician, King. It seems like there isn’t a name worthy of a man of his reputation. Something so bold, yet ethereal, and also remember that it isn't born a royal so that scratches Alexander, William and Charles of the list. What about Duncan? Kenneth? Is that Scottish enough? I could cheat a little and drop a line saying that he is part English on his mothers side and that could be part of the reason he has such disdain. Some superfluous details like his mother left him when he was young. King S could be a bastard! Or is that too Game of Thrones-esk.

Also this does ebb the flow of the story so it will likely be one of those things tacitly expressed to the reader instead of wasting my word count with the intricacies. The power of a name. Since he is a decorated war hero, he should also have a sobriquet affix to his title. King S, the worthy – no forget that. That’s rubbish. King S, the mighty, the great, the bold? I feel like some of these have been overused as epithets for kings in real wars. It has to be something original. Since he is self declared King of Scotland, I can eschew his sanctimonious attributes if he is picking his own epithets. The reader can deduce the King’s pretentious tendencies through his actions instead of his speech. King S, the righteous. Hmm. I’ll keep thinking.

Chapter 16 - The Time to Start

13/03/2024

When is the right time to start writing? I have painted a vivid picture of the characters in my head. Their attitudes and idiosyncrasies towards each other and their subjects. They are coming off the page almost in a tangible form. I am overhearing conversations on the metro and can hear Queen Annabelle’s witty retort. The way she curtails her steadfast royal zeal to remain composted and guard her true feelings. Yes. She speaks with an educated tongue but not in an ostentatious way. After all she is the Queen of England and is not a malevolent ruler. She can learn that from her father. Follow in his footsteps of bestowing aid and funds to the lower echelons of her kingdoms. Is this too fairytale? It does cut through that metaphorical red tape which I had wrapped the Queen up in. The way I have described her here makes her likeable and amiable which is what I want. That makes it even more heart wrenching if she is in fact the one who meets her premature death at the end. Young and sweet. They always make the best martyrs. Back to the question. Should I write about the opening encounter between the two monarchs in the next few days. I roughly have an idea on what they should say. Also the affectation in which they should say it. It is an important encounter as it sets the stage for the rest of the story. Their relationship hasn’t turned into a perturbed vituperation at this point.

My worry is I’ll write this early exchange and then I’ll find a gaping plot hole later on in the story. Get wrapped up in the why instead of the how. Without a solid lead for an ending, I would be writing blind, so-to-speak. In addition, I still need to decide on tertiary characters. It will be an incredibly linear story if it is just backwards and forwards between the two monarchs. Because of the Queen’s youthful appendage, I think it makes perfect sense for her to have an aide-de-camp. An advisor to – well advise. They could be someone who was inherited from her fathers staff and watched Annabelle grow up. Sort of like an uncle who isn’t an uncle but is referred to as an uncle. Does that make sense? The bond between them could be almost love but in a maternal sort of way. That might be worth exploring. I need to nail down the specifics of the entire plot before going off on a tangent. I guess that has answered my question. I am hoping that once I have delineated each part, the pieces will nicely slot together. Then it’s just a case of choosing the correct words to succinctly move the plot forwards. Of course that’s easier said than done.

Chapter 17 - A Clever or Easy Ending?

Instead of repeating myself using the same phrases such as ‘Queen Annabelle remained unperturbed at the King’s aloof demeanour’, I replaced these with ‘The Queen maintained the King’s gaze, unwilling to submit to intimidation’. Adds better flow and by adding body language, it helps the reader actually picture what is going on instead of reading an interview between two politicians. I have tried to keep the pacing in mind, as this is a short story, everything needs to transpire at lightning pace. I added a short scene in Queen Annabelle’s bed chamber as mentioned in a previous post to ground the reader and develop a few plot points. Apart from that ephemeral scene, each scene is full of zeal. Exciting. Some days the words just dance onto the page. I know what the salient dialogue points will be; I can build the scene around them and build upwards from there. The scene which is proving to be the most difficult is the opening … I can’t seem to get the right tone to introduce the Queen and Lady Sarah. Meh, I’ll come back to this another day.

Chapter 33 - Going with the Flow

07/04/2024

Writing, writing, writing. I’ve been a force of nature today. I’ve had 101 ideas bubbling away in my head. My main conflict was do I still with the plan or do I go off on a tangent. I don’t know! I have done a little of both, there are a few nice call backs and plenty of suspense and of course one can’t have suspense without betrayal. I’ve tidied up scenes 1, 2 and 4. The reason I have written scene 4 out of order is because it is a little scene which further adds to the relationship between the Queen and her aid. It add the backstory to the war of Scottish Independence which isn’t paramount to the plot but good to have. If I end up dwelling on the siege scene, scene 4 will be the first to go unfortunately, which will be a real shame because I really like it. The way I’ve written it makes the scene seamlessly flow. It’s beautiful. My writing is top tier when I feel like that. Anyway on to today’s progress. I was able to complete scene 3 which is when the King is deceiving the nobles back in the Scottish highlands. I didn’t know how that scene would turn out but I think after several hours of work, I have managed to capture the contrast between the King’s opulence and his subjects' indigence. I want the dichotomy to be striking so the readers know exactly what King Finlay’s character traits are. Unfortunately, there isn’t much showing when it comes to the King’s character in this scene but I think I have adequately shown his personal inclination when interlocking with the Queen in the previous scene. I have started on the siege – scene 5 – where the majority of it is just description. No dialogue as I think it cheapens the scene and takes the reader out of the moment if I stop and create a conversation between the queen and her guards. It was a real challenge having to carry a scene and drive it forward using a plethora of different adjectives. Throughout writing this story, I have realised that I use synonymous adjectives when describing the characters. When Queen Annabelle is listening to King Finlay speak, she is normally purposely holding his gaze and acting stoically. There is only a certain amount of times I can repeat words. I certainly cannot repeat words in the same scene. I have used palpable many times as well. Normally portraying the Queen’s discontentment or the King’s anomalous behaviour. These will be replaced with alternatives during the editing stage.

Chapter 34 - Valjean & Javert: Writing the Perfect Confrontation

08/04/2024

The confrontation is proving to be a source of constant consternation for me; to the point where it is keeping me awake at night. Lady Sarah is the Queen’s aid and also King Finlay’s daughter. Should scene 5 culminate with both women being captured and then scene 6 has King Finlay taking the chains of his daughter. It’s a crap way to unveil the big reveal but I don’t have anything better as of yet. I wanted to portray Lady Sarah as an aloof character but after serving Queen Annabelle for close to six years, I don’t think this is possible for her to have been playing a part for so long without getting emotionally attached to her Queen. She will always share her father’s politics – or maybe not his politics, but definitely offer her perpetual support – so it will be more accurate if Lady Sarah then sheds at least one tear for the Queen. It may be more poignant if I leave this reveal till the last scene. The King’s character has been built in the previous four scenes as a power hungry, manipulative, war hero and now I am expecting the readers to believe that actually he is sentimental and caring for his daughter? I need to show that the King has a human side without destroying his character from the rest of the story. I have written the first draft for the execution scene as well. That doesn’t hold any new information, it just serves as an ending to the story.

After days of ruminating how the Queen will die, hanging, guillotine, drowning, I have decided on good old fashioned decapitation via an executioner. Just so I can include the dialogue: Off with her head!. The King sat on the throne with his daughter – now the princess of England and Scotland – watching the Queen’s death. The final line is difficult to write. It needs to almost summarise the Queen’s reign but not be a victim to retelling the story. I would like the Queen’s memory to go on but in reality, she has only been on the throne for a meagre few days. Weeks at the very most. She hasn’t done anything significant. She has mourned her father and then foolishly informed the King of her naivety and gullible inclinations! I would like to give her a moniker because all fabled monarchs have one but it needs to be the right moniker to fit her character. It can’t be something generic like the undefeated or the worthy because that isn’t really her. I’ll keep thinking. Once I have decided on the big reveal for scene five - I will need to make sure that the execution scene leads on fluently. If it’s disjointed, the story will suffer. I need to keep the word count in mind as well because I am pushing on 2500 words and 500 words for the most important scene in the story may be problematic when it comes to editing. The first confrontation which sets the whole chain of events into motion came in at 810 words which is just under a third of the overall story length. Scenes 3 and 4 are shorter as they just provide additional details surrounding the King’s backstory and the Queen’s father’s backstory. Although nice to have. I think scene 4, where the Queen is reminiscing about her father may have to be truncated to make additional room for the confrontation. That, after all, is the pièce de résistance of the story.

Chapter 35 - Smart or Dumb Writing

09/04/2024

I was racking my brain all day trying to think of the best way for the King to recognise his daughter. I had hit a dead end – not quite a writer's block but certainly a tabula rasa which I couldn’t circumvent. I put off writing for most of the day until I made the decision to start writing, metaphorically down a dark alley. Almost as soon as I sat down, I thought of a cunning scene. I’m an idiot. If only I sat down to write earlier. Sometimes the best thing to do is just write. Write, even if deep down you know this is going to be bad. It just gets the ideas on paper. That normally sparks some other ideas and hopefully one will be useful. So, during the battle, Lady Sarah will be in the great hall with the Queen until the Queen sends her away. Annabelle thinks she is being kind by offering Sarah the chance to save herself but by doing this, she is actually instigating her own downfall. Sarah leaves the great hall and opens the gates to the palace, allowing the Scottish army in. This removes all lingering doubts about where Lady Sarah stands in relation to her nationalist values. Of course this isn’t going to be implicitly mentioned in the Siege scene, I’m going to save that for the confrontation so I have yet another reveal to work in.

By telling Lady Sarah to leave, it allows me to get her to enter the main chambers when the Queen has been captured in scene 6. First, the queen will be dismayed that the Scots have captured her aid, and then she will be in anguish upon coming to the realisation that she is indeed part Scottish. Then the betrayal becomes clear as the King embraces her and introduces her as his daughter. I sometimes get lost when deciding what should be explained unequivocally to the audience and which should be implicitly assumed. Of course showing instead of telling is ideal however with the constraints of a 3000 word story, I need to find the right balance because if I showed the audience everything such as King Finlay’s upbringing and the history surrounding the Scottish Independence war then this story would be closer to 10,000 words. Sometimes I need to be succinct but the key is knowing when. As I’m writing the confrontation, I am running out of my word count so I’ve had to make some cuts to the scene. I wanted to include the church in the discussion about King Finlay deciding to usurp Annabelle’s throne but that has been removed because I simply don’t have the room for it. It almost slows down the narrative too much when the King is proclaiming his plans and engaging in a polemic meeting between himself and the Queen. It makes no sense for the church to suddenly interject and introduce yet another subplot to the story. My initial idea was for the church to acquiesce to the King because he can offer them funds to buy their loyalty but that has had to be scrapped. Instead I have just thrown in a few throw away lines when the Queen is clutching at straws to win one over on the King before her execution. I particularly like “The Church will never agree to a heresy on the throne!”. The King comes back with a clever retort which sums up his character exquisitely but you’ll have to read the full story to discern that conversation.

Chapter 36 - A Skill Developed but Never Mastered

10/04/2024

Writing is an art, not a skill. There is no such thing as a good writer. Of course there are venerated writers who have a myriad of credits to their names who are regarded as a safe pair of hands. For instance, I would be comfortable handing money over to Russell T Davies to write a gay or sci-fi series (or gay sci-fi; he’s not done that yet,) or Guy Ritchie to write a gangster series. (Let's be honest, Guy Ritchie is the only reason Vinnie Jones is still an actor.) But back to the point, the more credits you accumulate, the more likely you are to be successful. Even if the TV are mostly below par, as long as they appeal to one demographic. It can be as niche as you want. There are some writers which I have enjoyed but haven’t received the proverbial recognition deserved. That is simply because they appeal to me and my genre of work. Writing is an art rather than a skill. It is tacitly learnt instead of explicitly taught. The subjective nature of writing can make it the best job in the world and also the most rewarding. To sit down and think of the weird and wonderful things that could or could not happen and turn them into works of fiction should be the only impetus anyone needs to start writing. However this is also the reason people don’t choose to write. They think they aren’t good enough or they don’t have a voice or they don’t have a story. All of this is redundant, I think. The most important thing about being a writer is actually being disciplined enough to sit down and write. It is inevitable that the first draft will be awful, but editing it into a piece that really speaks to people is part of the joy of being creative.

Chapter 37 - A Brief Update

16/04/2024

Six days have passed since I last updated you. I have completed a full draft and I have received some initial criticisms from the few people who have read it. The quality of the writing was commendable but the main concern was finding out when this chain of events happened. I’m not giving each scene a chapter title, instead I am inserting an asterisk between each paragraph to signify the changing of the setting. However I am leaving it up to the reader to discern the century that this is supposedly taking place in. That’s fine, however, the scenes have no time scale and I don’t explicitly mention how much time has passed between each scene. For instance, I have the Queen reminiscing about her father’s leadership in scene three and then directly following on for then, the siege is beginning. Is this the same night? Is the Queen aware of battle commencing? I am looking at adding an additional few lines, just delineating the proper timeline so it doesn’t seem as disjointed. This is rather difficult, only because of the word limit – after the first draft, I was on 3134 words. I’m allowed to go over the limit by 10% so I am fine for now. Future edits, I might find myself having to cut sub-plots out to save words.

I have effectively captured the essence of the characters which is great – this was deduced by my readers feedback, I’m not just being hubris. I have also changed the arrangement of the scenes to make it flow better. Previously, I had the scene with King Finlay back in Scotland as the third scene after his conference with Queen Annabelle. This left that additional scene with Annabelle reminiscing of her father with Lady Sarah as the fourth scene and following that we go straight into battle. I thought it would be better if the Queen and Lady Sarah had their intimate chat straight after the confrontation so that could potentially be happening later that day. Also because I haven’t explicitly said what century this is set in but tacitly implied it’s mediaeval times, it would take a few days – if not weeks – for King Finlay to get to and from the palace. Putting the King Finlay scene where he’s back in Scotland after the initial confrontation is ideal because it gives the appearance of being some time later. At the end of that scene as well, I have added an additional few lines for King Finlay to say which declares war on the English. That then nicely follows on to the next scene which is the siege on the English. The potentially gaping plot hole is, does Queen Annabelle know that the Scottish are coming to invade. Does she receive word? I need to add some sort of explanation because currently, scene five has the Queen primed and ready to fight. It jumps straight into the action because I don’t have the luxury of circumventing the word count limit and filling in the gaps.

It might be inconsequential when looking at the story holistically but I don’t want there to be any disparities between what the reader knows and what the characters know. Last thing. All mention of the church and their influence on the King’s decisions have been removed. It’s a nice detail to have but in the end, it doesn’t add anything to the plot. The ecclesiastical nobles were the main competition in disputing the forthcoming war but now I’ve changed it to an brave anonymous noble who raises concerns over the ramifications of another war. My initial plan was to cut Queen Annabelle’s scene in her bed chambers where she is telling Lady Sarah about her father and his regrets over the way he handled the Scottish polemic voicing independence. After days of mulling it over, this scene actually adds to the overall story and changes the dynamic between Lady Sarah and her inclination towards the Queen and the late King James. I am currently at 3261 words so I am still on the right side of the word limit.

Chapter 38 - First Draft Complete

17/04/2024

I am at that point where the story is done. I have reached my word limit, I cannot add any more detail without surpassing the demarcation. However, I have solicited my mother to read it over after the amendments and she has elucidated some important points. In the great hall when the revelations are starting to unfold, the King admits that Lady Sarah is well placed to influence the Queen’s decisions and carry out clandestine actions such as opening the gates to the Scottish armies and poisoning the King’s chalice. My mother astutely asked “How has Lady Sarah influenced the Queen”. She is still of paramount importance to the plot because of the physical actions she has undertaken to undermine the Queen but has she actually influence the Queen’s decisions. Of course, at the time, I equivocated and replied with, ‘It’s only 3000 words, I cannot explore every avenue of subterfuge on Lady Sarah behest’. However, looking back through the scenes involving Lady Sarah, I think I can inculcate certain decisions via Lady Sarah. How exciting.

I thought my story was almost finished but my brain is still ticking away. Now, in the first scene, instead of the tone of the scene focusing on the naivety of Queen Annabelle in summoning the King of Scotland, that suggestion is postulated by Lady Sarah, so now it is Lady Sarah’s fault that the King of Scotland came to England in the first place. She sets the entire chain of events into action. I mean, she did anyway because she poisoned the King, but the reader doesn’t know this yet. I was always keen on giving the reader subtle hints about Lady Sarah’s allegiance however I didn’t know how to do it. The new tone of the first scene allows me to incorporate her perfidy in a perfidious way, that the readers will not know of her true inclinations until the final scenes. However if they reread the story, they will be able to pick out her insidious actions. God, I’m a talented raconteur.

Chapter 39 - Coming to an End

18/04/2024

Now I almost have the finished story, I have been focused solely on tone and themes. Rereading my scenes to make sure that each character is portrayed in the meticulous way that I imagined. Each scene reveals something new and I made sure that the overall tone of the writing was consistent. Above all, readers of all ages and genres should be able to follow the plot. Before I started, I didn’t pay attention to the specific themes which I wanted to portray. It wasn’t contrived. It happened naturally as the story developed. The overall character arcs greatly dictated the running themes throughout, for instance, Lady Sarah’s betrayal, King Finlay’s lust for power and Queen Annabelle’s desire to be a good Queen. I am happy with how each character has developed and after many days of consternation, I’m satisfied at their endings as well. (Particularly with the death of Queen Annabelle – you know how much I wanted a public execution.) Now, delving deeper into the themes. First and foremost, leadership and sovereignty. Both Queen Annabelle and King Finlay grapple with the weight of their positions and the decisions they must make for the welfare of their kingdoms. The struggle for control and dominance underscores the theme of leadership throughout the narrative. Check.

Secondly, Betrayal and loyalty are central themes as characters navigate the murky waters of political intrigue and personal allegiance. Queen Annabelle experiences betrayal from those she trusted most, particularly Lady Sarah, who ultimately proves disloyal to her queen. Conversely, Annabelle's steadfast loyalty to her father and her kingdom remains unwavering until her untimely demise. Check. Thirdly, the story explores the consequences of conflict and war, both on a personal and societal level. The animosity between Queen Annabelle and King Finlay escalates into a full-blown conflict, resulting in the siege of the royal palace and ultimately Annabelle's execution. The toll of war on individuals and nations alike is portrayed through vivid descriptions of battle and the aftermath of conquest. Check. Fourthly, identity and legacy are significant themes as characters grapple with their roles and responsibilities in shaping the future of their kingdoms. Queen Annabelle strives to uphold her father's legacy while forging her own path as a leader. Meanwhile, King Finlay's actions are driven by a desire to solidify his own legacy, regardless of the cost to others. Check.

Last but not least, the theme of justice and morality runs throughout the story, as characters confront ethical dilemmas and wrestle with the consequences of their actions. Queen Annabelle's steadfast belief in justice and her commitment to her principles ultimately lead to her downfall, while King Finlay's Machiavellian approach to governance raises questions about the morality of his reign. Check. Overall I think I have covered all the heart wrenching themes which I was broadly aiming for. Some writer need to note down every aspect of the story to give them a definitive guide to stick to, however this just isn’t my way of writing. I let the themes play out naturally, that way I wasn’t forcing anything and I could further refine the themes once I had at least the first draft completed. Now bear with me whilst I digress, and I know fine well that I am getting ahead of myself here but I think it is interesting to ruminate about, if it was adapted for the screen would it be a melodrama or a drama. What’s the difference you may ask? Well, as Michael Grade colloquially put it: a character hates his boyfriend, so he goes out, buys a gun, goes to his boyfriend’s house and pulls the trigger, but the gun jams. That’s a melodrama. It is the same for a drama, goes out, buys a gun, goes to his boyfriend’s house but he physically can’t pull the trigger because he’s too in love. That’s a drama. A drama which I may consider writing because that sounds intense! In the tale of sovereignty – how's that for a title? - I would describe it as a drama because although there is emotional conflict between the characters, the severity of the plot and its weighty themes which I’ve explained above contributes to its classification as a drama. The story’s intricate plot, well-developed characters and nuanced exploration of political intrigue and personal relationships neatly defines it as a drama.

Chapter 40 - Long Live Queen Annebelle

19/04/2024

So there it is, the end of my 3000 word short story. I hope you have got a thrill from my pages of rambling about structure, themes and plot details. Maybe even be inspired by the plethora of rejected ideas from this short story. The overwhelming lesson which I have learnt from this process is that writing is hard, but the most important parable to remember is it does get easier and the words will start flowing. Having the motivation to sit down and write, even if it is only a couple of sentences a day, will inevitably improve your craft. There are still many unanswered questions which will always have subjective answers. There is no right way of approaching a piece of writing. For this particular piece, I planned out every scene before I even wrote a word. I knew what was going to happen and when, which ultimately helped me structure my first story. Since finishing, I have started a new piece which I am writing spontaneously. No plan, no structure, no ending in sight and it is invigorating seeing where this process takes me. Of course, writer's block is our worst nemesis and self doubt. Unfortunately, that never goes away. Once one story ends, new characters, new plots and new themes are introduced in the next project. Therefore we need to keep our motivation when writing. It will all be worth it when we'll look back at our finished stories.
